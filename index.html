Key Bioavailability Insights

Practical takeaways from the research.

❄️ Frozen can beat "fresh"

Produce is flash-frozen within hours of harvest, locking in nutrients at peak levels. Store-bought "fresh" produce may have traveled for days, losing water-soluble vitamins to oxidation. After 5 days on a shelf, frozen vegetables are measurably more nutritious. [3, 8]

💧 Water leaches water-soluble nutrients

Boiling dissolves vitamin C, B vitamins, and potassium into cooking water through leaching. Up to 60% of vitamin C and potassium can migrate to the water. Steam or microwave instead, or use the cooking liquid in soups. [2, 5, 10]

🍎 Cooking can increase bioavailability

Heat breaks down plant cell walls, releasing trapped nutrients. Lycopene bioavailability in tomatoes increases 2–3x with cooking. Beta-carotene in carrots becomes far more bioaccessible when cooked with a small amount of fat. [7, 12]

⚡ Minerals are heat-stable but can leach

Iron, calcium, and potassium are not destroyed by heat. Their main loss is leaching into water, not chemical breakdown. Dry-heat methods (roasting, microwaving, stir-frying) preserve nearly all mineral content. [4, 6]

⏱️ Time + oxygen + light degrade vitamins

Vitamin C is oxidized by air and broken down by light. Fresh produce loses 10–25% of its vitamin C per week at room temperature. Folate is similarly oxygen-sensitive. Eat fresh produce quickly, or choose frozen. [1, 5]

🧀 Fat + fat-soluble vitamins = better absorption

Vitamins A, D, E, and K dissolve in fat, not water. Eating them with dietary fat (olive oil, butter, avocado) increases absorption through the intestinal wall by up to 5–10x. [11, 12]

Understanding Bioavailability

Not all the nutrients listed on a Nutrition Facts label make it into your body. Bioavailability is the proportion of a nutrient that is actually absorbed and used by your body after you eat it. Two people eating the same food may absorb very different amounts of the same nutrient, depending on how that food was stored, processed, and cooked. [11, 12]

Bioaccessibility

The fraction of a nutrient that is released from the food matrix during digestion so it could be absorbed. Cooking often increases bioaccessibility by breaking down plant cell walls, softening fibers, and denaturing proteins that trap nutrients inside cells. [11]

Bioavailability

The fraction that actually crosses the intestinal wall and enters circulation to be used by cells. This depends on bioaccessibility plus the nutrient's chemical form, interactions with other food components, and the person's gut health. [12]

Why Retention Alone Isn't the Whole Story

A food might retain 100% of a nutrient but have low bioavailability because the nutrient is locked inside tough cell walls. Conversely, cooking may reduce total content while increasing what your body actually absorbs — as with lycopene in tomatoes and beta-carotene in carrots. [7, 12]

Key Factors That Affect Bioavailability

Enhancers: Vitamin C boosts iron absorption. Fat increases absorption of vitamins A, D, E, K. Cooking breaks cell walls.
Inhibitors: Phytates (grains/legumes), oxalates (spinach), tannins (tea), and fiber can bind minerals and reduce absorption. [4, 11]
